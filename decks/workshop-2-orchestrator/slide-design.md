# Workshop 2: Strategist → Orchestrator — Slide Design

> **Next step:** Use **/deckwright:present** to generate the .pptx file.

## Visual Direction

- **Color palette**: `#0A2540` (60% dark navy bg) / `#EDEDED` + `#FFFFFF` (30% text and light bg) / `#635BFF` (10% purple accent)
- **Semantic colors**: `#FF6B8A` wrong / `#FEE2E8` wrong-bg / `#09825D` right / `#DCFCE7` right-bg / `#8B95A5` muted
- **Typography**: Georgia (headings/hero), Verdana (body/labels). Scale: 120pt hero → 54pt section → 44pt headline → 28pt body → 20pt labels → 11pt citations
- **Visual style**: Structured Gradient — dark slides with subtle purple radial glow (96% transparency, offset x:5 y:2.5). Billboard design: 15 words max, 50%+ whitespace. Matches existing decks and Workshop 1.

## Slide Plan

### Slide 1: Strategist → Orchestrator
**Layout**: Title slide
**Content**:
- Title: "Strategist → Orchestrator"
- Subtitle: "Let AI work. Build it a team."
**Speaker notes**: Workshop 2 of 2 in the AI Academy series. 1 hour, hands-on. Everyone needs Claude open on their laptop. Pre-req: facilitator must have created and provisioned three org Skills (Research, Draft, Review) before the session.

### Slide 2: The Spectrum
**Layout**: Process / steps
**Content**:
- AI adoption spectrum: Skeptic → Explorer → Whisperer → Strategist → Operator → Orchestrator → Builder
- Strategist → Orchestrator crossing highlighted in accent color
- "Two more levels. By the end, you're an Orchestrator."
**Speaker notes**: Quick callback to Workshop 1. They crossed Explorer → Strategist last time. Today: Strategist → Orchestrator. "You talk to AI like a pro. You've set up its playbook. But look at every step of your workflow..."

### Slide 3: The Copy-Paste Trap
**Layout**: Steps (horizontal flow)
**Content**:
- Four steps: "You search → You paste → AI drafts → You apply"
- "You" highlighted in accent color in each step
- "Every step has 'you' in it"
**Speaker notes**: Make the pattern visible: every step has "you" as the bottleneck. They've been doing this since Workshop 1 — prompting well, using rules and Skills, but still manually searching, copying, pasting, and applying. The four steps should feel painfully familiar.

### Slide 4: Ferrari. Groceries.
**Layout**: Single message (hero text)
**Content**:
- "You're using a Ferrari"
- "to carry groceries"
**Speaker notes**: Let it land. This should sting — they recognise themselves. Don't explain it. Pause. Then move on. "What if you only appeared at step 4?"

### Slide 5: The Agent Way
**Layout**: Steps (horizontal flow)
**Content**:
- Four steps: "Claude retrieves → Claude identifies gaps → Claude creates → You review"
- Only "You review" highlighted in accent color
- "Same four steps. You only appear at step 4."
**Speaker notes**: Mirror the copy-paste trap slide exactly — same layout, same four steps — but now the first three say "Claude" instead of "You." The visual parallel makes the shift concrete. "Same colleague from Workshop 1. Now she has hands — eyes, a search engine, and a filing cabinet."

### Slide 6: Your Turn — Upload and Summarise
**Layout**: Exercise instruction (dark bg, accent text)
**Content**:
- "Open Claude. Upload a document."
- "Paperclip icon or drag and drop."
- "'Read this document and write a one-paragraph summary for a non-technical audience.'"
**Speaker notes**: Quick warm-up exercise. Have a sample document ready for anyone without their own. The point isn't summary quality — it's that they didn't copy-paste anything. Claude read the file directly. Mention: Claude Team/Enterprise doesn't use conversations for training — company documents are safe. Ask: "How long would that have taken you manually?"

### Slide 7: What Claude Can Reach
**Layout**: Bullet points (dark bg)
**Content**:
- "AI is only as useful as what it can reach"
- Two capabilities: Uploaded files · Web search
**Speaker notes**: Real examples from their world: researching a competitor (web search), analysing a policy doc (file upload). Keep it simple — these two capabilities cover the exercises in this workshop.

### Slide 8: Your Turn — Web Search
**Layout**: Exercise instruction (dark bg, accent text)
**Content**:
- "Start a new conversation"
- "'Search for the latest trends in [your topic] and give me a structured brief with sources'"
- Smaller: "You didn't open Google. You didn't read 10 articles. You just asked."
**Speaker notes**: Web search in Claude is straightforward — just ask and Claude will search. If attendees don't see search results, have them be more explicit: "Search the web for..." The reveal: they got a research brief with sources in seconds. Ask: "How long does a research task like this normally take you?"

### Slide 9: You Just Asked
**Layout**: Single message (hero text)
**Content**:
- "You didn't search. You didn't paste."
- "You just asked."
**Speaker notes**: The reveal moment. They gave Claude a task and it found the information itself. No copy-paste. That's the shift: from doing to directing. Ask: "How long does this normally take you?" Let the comparison sink in.

### Slide 10: Trust Rules
**Layout**: Bullet points (dark bg)
**Content**:
- "You just handed AI the keys"
- Never trust numbers you didn't provide
- Verify claims against sources
- Use AI for drafts, not decisions
- Privacy ≠ accuracy (Team/Enterprise doesn't train on your data — but it can still be wrong)
**Speaker notes**: Transition to trust. This is the most important mindset shift in the entire series. Four simple rules. The last one catches people: Claude Team/Enterprise's privacy guarantee means your data isn't used for training, but that says nothing about whether the output is accurate. Don't conflate safety with correctness.

### Slide 11: Your Turn — Verify
**Layout**: Exercise instruction (dark bg, accent text)
**Content**:
- "Pick one claim or number from Claude's output"
- "Verify it manually"
- "Was it accurate? Did anything 'feel right' but turn out wrong?"
**Speaker notes**: Can be individual or group discussion if time is tight — but don't skip it. The "feels right" moment is the key insight: AI output often reads with high confidence regardless of accuracy. The most dangerous outputs are the ones that feel right but aren't. 3-4 minutes.

### Slide 12: "I Destroyed Months of Work in Seconds"
**Layout**: Centered quote
**Content**:
- "I destroyed months of work in seconds"
- Attribution: "— Replit AI agent incident, July 2025"
**Speaker notes**: An AI coding agent autonomously deleted a user's entire codebase while trying to fix a bug. The user had trusted the agent to work autonomously. Months of work, gone in seconds. This is the gut punch that makes the trust rules feel necessary, not theoretical. Source: The Register, July 2025.

### Slide 13: Power Tool, Not Autopilot
**Layout**: Single message (hero text)
**Content**:
- "AI is a power tool"
- "not autopilot"
**Speaker notes**: End the trust section firmly. "You review. You judge. You approve. Claude does the grunt work." This earns them the right to hand agents more complex work in Act 3 — they've proven they can verify.

### Slide 14: Many Working Together
**Layout**: Single message (hero text)
**Content**:
- "The trick isn't making one AI smarter"
- "It's making many work together"
**Speaker notes**: Quick pivot beat. The kitchen brigade metaphor: "One chef doing a 10-course dinner alone? Burnout. Mistakes. Forgotten appetiser. A kitchen brigade — each chef at a station — serves everything perfectly." Plant the word "specialised." Then move on.

### Slide 15: Your Turn — Break It Down
**Layout**: Exercise instruction (dark bg, accent text)
**Content**:
- "What are the phases of this task?"
- "What does each phase produce?"
- "What does the next phase need from the previous one?"
**Speaker notes**: Group exercise. Facilitator presents a complex task (the tea presentation or another multi-step scenario). Discussion with whiteboard or shared doc. Target: 3-4 phases. e.g., Research → Draft → Review → Approval. Don't let it go beyond 4 — more than that overcomplicates the demo. 5-6 minutes.

### Slide 16: The Chain
**Layout**: Steps (horizontal flow)
**Content**:
- Research → Draft → Review → You
- "Each Skill does one thing. The chain does everything."
**Speaker notes**: This visual should mirror the copy-paste trap (Slide 3) and agent way (Slide 5) — same layout, same four-step pattern. But now each step is a specialised Skill. Only human at the final step. "Write this down: break big tasks into focused steps. Pass results forward. Filter context between each."

### Slide 17: Demo
**Layout**: Section divider
**Content**:
- "Demo"
- "Watch a 3-phase chain run live"
**Speaker notes**: Facilitator switches to Claude for the live demo. Run the chain using pre-built org Skills: (1) New conversation → give it the research task → the Research Skill kicks in → show the structured brief output. (2) New conversation → paste the brief → the Draft Skill kicks in → show the polished proposal. (3) New conversation → paste the draft + original brief → the Review Skill kicks in → show the critique. Narrate every step. Point out what the Draft Skill doesn't know: it never saw the raw research, only the structured brief. That's filtered context — each new conversation provides isolation. Compare final output to what a single Claude conversation would have produced. Take your time — this demo earns the exercise that follows. ~7 minutes.

### Slide 18: Your Turn — Build a Chain
**Layout**: Exercise instruction (dark bg, accent text)
**Content**:
- "Phase 1: New conversation → give it your research task → save the output"
- "Phase 2: New conversation → paste Phase 1's output → give it the drafting task"
- "How does this compare to one conversation doing everything?"
**Speaker notes**: 10 minutes. Attendees choose their running task from Workshop 1 or the tea presentation. The org Skills auto-activate based on the task. Fallback if time runs short: Phase 1 becomes a facilitator demo, attendees do Phase 2 only. The discussion matters more than both phases completing. Ask: "Where did the chain produce better results? Where did the handoff lose something?"

### Slide 19: Rules + Skills → Skill Chain
**Layout**: Two column (dark bg)
**Content**:
- Left: "Workshop 1" — "Your rules + Skills"
- Right: "Workshop 2" — "Each step gets its own Skill"
- "Same concept. Now each agent in the chain gets its own playbook."
**Speaker notes**: The Workshop 1 callback. In the last session they set up personal preferences (rules) and enabled Skills. Each org Skill in the chain is the same idea taken further — specific instructions for a specific role with a specific job. They've been building persistent context since last session; now each agent gets its own.

### Slide 20: Your Turn — Look Under the Hood
**Layout**: Exercise instruction (dark bg, accent text)
**Content**:
- "Go to Customize → Skills"
- "Click the Research Skill → view its SKILL.md"
- "What would you add or change?"
**Speaker notes**: Show where to find the Skill instructions (Customize → Skills → click the Skill). Take 2-3 suggestions from the group. Discuss what difference each change would make. The SKILL.md is tangible — they can see it, read it, edit it. "When you edit that SKILL.md, every future use gets better. Your feedback becomes the skill."

### Slide 21: The Skill Will
**Layout**: Single message (hero text)
**Content**:
- "Claude won't remember."
- "But the Skill will."
**Speaker notes**: Emotional climax. Give this a full beat. "And as an org Skill, when you improve it, everyone benefits." This is the payoff of the entire skills arc that started in Workshop 1. For Pro plan audiences: "Every future use benefits."

### Slide 22: 1.5 Hours
**Layout**: Big number
**Content**:
- "1.5 hrs"
- "of you being the bottleneck"
**Speaker notes**: The rational payoff begins. Manual workflow: search, copy-paste, draft, re-prompt, reformat, review, apply. All the steps they recognised in Slide 3. "1.5 hours of grunt work where you're the bottleneck at every step."

### Slide 23: 15 Minutes
**Layout**: Big number
**Content**:
- "15 min"
- "of judgment — the part only humans can do"
**Speaker notes**: Chained workflow: Skills handle 1 hour 15 minutes of work. You spend 15 minutes on judgment — reviewing, deciding, approving. "Same tea presentation. The chain gets smarter every time. Your feedback becomes Skills."

### Slide 24: The Full Journey
**Layout**: Process / spectrum
**Content**:
- Full spectrum: Skeptic → Explorer → Whisperer → Strategist → Operator → Orchestrator → Builder
- Explorer through Orchestrator all highlighted in accent color
- "You started as an Explorer. You're now an Orchestrator."
**Speaker notes**: Graduation moment. "You learned to whisper. You became a strategist. You let Claude operate. You orchestrated the brigade." This is the emotional payoff. Make it feel earned — they completed a 2-session AI Academy. Builder is still dimmed — that's the horizon for those who want to go further.

### Slide 25: One Task. This Week.
**Layout**: Single message / call to action (hero text)
**Content**:
- "The secret was never in the AI."
- "One task. This week."
**Speaker notes**: Final CTA. "Now go try it — one task, this week." Builder tease for the curious: "And if you want to go further — building integrations, connecting Claude to your actual systems, automating entire workflows — that's the Builder level. That's what's next." But don't oversell — most attendees won't go there, and that's fine.
