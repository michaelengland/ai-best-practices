# Workshop 2: Strategist → Orchestrator — Slide Design

> **Next step:** Use **/deckwright:present** to generate the .pptx file.

## Visual Direction

- **Color palette**: `#0A2540` (60% dark navy bg) / `#EDEDED` + `#FFFFFF` (30% text and light bg) / `#635BFF` (10% purple accent)
- **Semantic colors**: `#FF6B8A` wrong / `#FEE2E8` wrong-bg / `#09825D` right / `#DCFCE7` right-bg / `#8B95A5` muted
- **Typography**: Georgia (headings/hero), Verdana (body/labels). Scale: 120pt hero → 54pt section → 44pt headline → 28pt body → 20pt labels → 11pt citations
- **Visual style**: Structured Gradient — dark slides with subtle purple radial glow (96% transparency, offset x:5 y:2.5). Billboard design: 15 words max, 50%+ whitespace. Matches existing decks and Workshop 1.

## Slide Plan

### Slide 1: Strategist → Orchestrator
**Layout**: Title slide
**Content**:
- Title: "Strategist → Orchestrator"
- Subtitle: "Let AI work. Build it a team."
**Speaker notes**: Workshop 2 of 2 in the AI Academy series. 1 hour, hands-on. Everyone needs ChatGPT Org open on their laptop. Pre-req: facilitator must have enabled Gmail/apps in Workspace Settings → Apps and created three Custom GPTs (Research, Draft, Review) published to the org workspace.

### Slide 2: The Spectrum
**Layout**: Process / steps
**Content**:
- AI adoption spectrum: Skeptic → Explorer → Whisperer → Strategist → Operator → Orchestrator → Builder
- Strategist → Orchestrator crossing highlighted in accent color
- "Two more levels. By the end, you're an Orchestrator."
**Speaker notes**: Quick callback to Workshop 1. They crossed Explorer → Strategist last time. Today: Strategist → Orchestrator. "You talk to AI like a pro. You've set up its playbook. But look at every step of your workflow..."

### Slide 3: The Copy-Paste Trap
**Layout**: Steps (horizontal flow)
**Content**:
- Four steps: "You search → You paste → AI drafts → You apply"
- "You" highlighted in accent color in each step
- "Every step has 'you' in it"
**Speaker notes**: Make the pattern visible: every step has "you" as the bottleneck. They've been doing this since Workshop 1 — prompting well, using skills, but still manually searching, copying, pasting, and applying. The four steps should feel painfully familiar.

### Slide 4: Ferrari. Groceries.
**Layout**: Single message (hero text)
**Content**:
- "You're using a Ferrari"
- "to carry groceries"
**Speaker notes**: Let it land. This should sting — they recognise themselves. Don't explain it. Pause. Then move on. "What if you only appeared at step 4?"

### Slide 5: The Agent Way
**Layout**: Steps (horizontal flow)
**Content**:
- Four steps: "Agent retrieves → Agent identifies gaps → Agent creates → You review"
- Only "You review" highlighted in accent color
- "Same four steps. You only appear at step 4."
**Speaker notes**: Mirror the copy-paste trap slide exactly — same layout, same four steps — but now the first three say "Agent" instead of "You." The visual parallel makes the shift concrete. "Same colleague from Workshop 1. Now she has hands — eyes, a phone, and a to-do list."

### Slide 6: Your Turn — Upload and Summarise
**Layout**: Exercise instruction (dark bg, accent text)
**Content**:
- "Open ChatGPT. Upload a document."
- "Paperclip icon or drag and drop."
- "'Read this document and write a one-paragraph summary for a non-technical audience.'"
**Speaker notes**: Quick warm-up exercise. Have a sample document ready for anyone without their own. The point isn't summary quality — it's that they didn't copy-paste anything. ChatGPT read the file directly. Mention: ChatGPT Org doesn't use conversations for training — company documents are safe. Ask: "How long would that have taken you manually?"

### Slide 7: What an Agent Can Reach
**Layout**: Bullet points (dark bg)
**Content**:
- "An agent is only as useful as what it can reach"
- Six connections: Email · Calendar · Files · Chat · Web · Custom
**Speaker notes**: Real examples from their world: Monday morning email triage (Email), meeting prep from calendar invites (Calendar), finding that doc someone mentioned in Slack (Chat + Files), researching a competitor (Web). These aren't hypothetical — they're the tasks that eat their mornings.

### Slide 8: Your Turn — Agent Mode
**Layout**: Exercise instruction (dark bg, accent text)
**Content**:
- "Switch to agent mode:"
- "Tools menu → Agent (or type /agent)"
- "'@Gmail Summarise my last 5 unread emails and flag anything that needs a response today'"
**Speaker notes**: Pre-req: Gmail must be enabled in Workspace Settings → Apps by admin. Verify this works before the session — the UI changes periodically. Walk the room. If anyone can't access agent mode, have them pair with a neighbour. The exercise should take 2-3 minutes.

### Slide 9: You Just Asked
**Layout**: Single message (hero text)
**Content**:
- "You didn't search. You didn't paste."
- "You just asked."
**Speaker notes**: The reveal moment. They gave the agent access to their inbox, and it did the work. No copy-paste. That's the shift: from doing to directing. Ask: "How long does this normally take you on a Monday morning?" Let the comparison sink in.

### Slide 10: Trust Rules
**Layout**: Bullet points (dark bg)
**Content**:
- "You just handed AI the keys"
- Never trust numbers you didn't provide
- Verify claims against sources
- Use AI for drafts, not decisions
- Privacy ≠ accuracy (Org doesn't train on your data — but it can still be wrong)
**Speaker notes**: Transition to trust. This is the most important mindset shift in the entire series. Four simple rules. The last one catches people: ChatGPT Org's privacy guarantee means your data isn't used for training, but that says nothing about whether the output is accurate. Don't conflate safety with correctness.

### Slide 11: Your Turn — Verify
**Layout**: Exercise instruction (dark bg, accent text)
**Content**:
- "Pick one claim or number from the agent's output"
- "Verify it manually"
- "Was it accurate? Did anything 'feel right' but turn out wrong?"
**Speaker notes**: Can be individual or group discussion if time is tight — but don't skip it. The "feels right" moment is the key insight: AI output often reads with high confidence regardless of accuracy. The most dangerous outputs are the ones that feel right but aren't. 3-4 minutes.

### Slide 12: "I Destroyed Months of Work in Seconds"
**Layout**: Centered quote
**Content**:
- "I destroyed months of work in seconds"
- Attribution: "— Replit AI agent incident, July 2025"
**Speaker notes**: An AI coding agent autonomously deleted a user's entire codebase while trying to fix a bug. The user had trusted the agent to work autonomously. Months of work, gone in seconds. This is the gut punch that makes the trust rules feel necessary, not theoretical. Source: The Register, July 2025.

### Slide 13: Power Tool, Not Autopilot
**Layout**: Single message (hero text)
**Content**:
- "AI is a power tool"
- "not autopilot"
**Speaker notes**: End the trust section firmly. "You review. You judge. You approve. The agent does the grunt work." This earns them the right to hand agents more complex work in Act 3 — they've proven they can verify.

### Slide 14: Many Working Together
**Layout**: Single message (hero text)
**Content**:
- "The trick isn't making one AI smarter"
- "It's making many work together"
**Speaker notes**: Quick pivot beat. The kitchen brigade metaphor: "One chef doing a 10-course dinner alone? Burnout. Mistakes. Forgotten appetiser. A kitchen brigade — each chef at a station — serves everything perfectly." Plant the word "specialised." Then move on.

### Slide 15: Your Turn — Break It Down
**Layout**: Exercise instruction (dark bg, accent text)
**Content**:
- "What are the phases of this task?"
- "What does each phase produce?"
- "What does the next phase need from the previous one?"
**Speaker notes**: Group exercise. Facilitator presents a complex task (the onboarding proposal or another multi-step scenario). Discussion with whiteboard or shared doc. Target: 3-4 phases. e.g., Research → Draft → Review → Approval. Don't let it go beyond 4 — more than that overcomplicates the demo. 5-6 minutes.

### Slide 16: The Chain
**Layout**: Steps (horizontal flow)
**Content**:
- Research → Draft → Review → You
- "Each agent does one thing. The chain does everything."
**Speaker notes**: This visual should mirror the copy-paste trap (Slide 3) and agent way (Slide 5) — same layout, same four-step pattern. But now each step is a specialised agent. Only human at the final step. "Write this down: break big tasks into focused steps. Pass results forward. Filter context between each."

### Slide 17: Demo
**Layout**: Section divider
**Content**:
- "Demo"
- "Watch a 3-phase chain run live"
**Speaker notes**: Facilitator switches to ChatGPT for the live demo. Run the chain using pre-built Custom GPTs: (1) Open Research Agent → give it the task → show the structured brief output. (2) Open Draft Agent → paste the brief → show the polished proposal. (3) Open Review Agent → paste the draft + original brief → show the critique. Narrate every step. Point out what Draft Agent doesn't know: it never saw the raw research, only the structured brief. That's filtered context. Compare final output to what a single ChatGPT conversation would have produced. Take your time — this demo earns the exercise that follows. ~7 minutes.

### Slide 18: Your Turn — Build a Chain
**Layout**: Exercise instruction (dark bg, accent text)
**Content**:
- "Phase 1: Open Research Agent → give it your task → save the output"
- "Phase 2: Open Draft Agent → paste Phase 1's output → give it the drafting task"
- "How does this compare to one conversation doing everything?"
**Speaker notes**: 10 minutes. Attendees choose their running task from Workshop 1 or the prescribed one. Custom GPTs are in the ChatGPT sidebar. Fallback if time runs short: Phase 1 becomes a facilitator demo, attendees do Phase 2 only. The discussion matters more than both phases completing. Ask: "Where did the chain produce better results? Where did the handoff lose something?"

### Slide 19: Skills — Now Each Agent Gets Its Own
**Layout**: Two column (dark bg)
**Content**:
- Left: "Workshop 1" — "Your custom instructions"
- Right: "Workshop 2" — "Each agent's system prompt"
- "Same concept. Now each agent in the chain gets its own focused skill."
**Speaker notes**: The Workshop 1 callback. In the last session they set up custom instructions for themselves. Each Custom GPT is the same idea — but for a specific agent with a specific job. They've been building skills since last session; now each agent gets its own.

### Slide 20: Your Turn — Look Under the Hood
**Layout**: Exercise instruction (dark bg, accent text)
**Content**:
- "Open the Research Agent"
- "Click the name → view system prompt"
- "What would you add or change?"
**Speaker notes**: Show where to find the system prompt (click Custom GPT name → configure). Take 2-3 suggestions from the group. Discuss what difference each change would make. The system prompt is tangible — they can see it, read it, edit it. "When you edit that prompt, every future run gets better. Your feedback becomes the skill."

### Slide 21: The Skill Will
**Layout**: Single message (hero text)
**Content**:
- "The agent won't remember."
- "But the skill will."
**Speaker notes**: Emotional climax. Give this a full beat. "And in ChatGPT Org, when you improve a shared agent, everyone benefits." This is the payoff of the entire skills arc that started in Workshop 1.

### Slide 22: 1.5 Hours
**Layout**: Big number
**Content**:
- "1.5 hrs"
- "of you being the bottleneck"
**Speaker notes**: The rational payoff begins. Manual workflow: search, copy-paste, draft, re-prompt, reformat, review, apply. All the steps they recognised in Slide 3. "1.5 hours of grunt work where you're the bottleneck at every step."

### Slide 23: 15 Minutes
**Layout**: Big number
**Content**:
- "15 min"
- "of judgment — the part only humans can do"
**Speaker notes**: Chained workflow: agents handle 1 hour 15 minutes of work. You spend 15 minutes on judgment — reviewing, deciding, approving. "Same proposal. The chain gets smarter every time. Your feedback becomes skills."

### Slide 24: The Full Journey
**Layout**: Process / spectrum
**Content**:
- Full spectrum: Skeptic → Explorer → Whisperer → Strategist → Operator → Orchestrator → Builder
- Explorer through Orchestrator all highlighted in accent color
- "You started as an Explorer. You're now an Orchestrator."
**Speaker notes**: Graduation moment. "You learned to whisper. You became a strategist. You let agents operate. You orchestrated the brigade." This is the emotional payoff. Make it feel earned — they completed a 2-session AI Academy. Builder is still dimmed — that's the horizon for those who want to go further.

### Slide 25: One Task. This Week.
**Layout**: Single message / call to action (hero text)
**Content**:
- "The secret was never in the AI."
- "One task. This week."
**Speaker notes**: Final CTA. Same as the reference deck. "Now go try it — one task, this week." Builder tease for the curious: "And if you want to go further — building plugins, connecting agents to your actual systems, automating entire workflows — that's the Builder level. That's what's next." But don't oversell — most attendees won't go there, and that's fine.
