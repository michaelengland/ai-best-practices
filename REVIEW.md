## Key observation

The workshops are **extractive** — each one takes a single persona crossing from the main deck (~10–15 teaching slides), strips out everything before and after, and wraps those slides in exercise scaffolding. No contradictions. The borrowed content is always the conceptual core (wrong/right comparisons, metaphors, stats); the new content is always hands-on and tool-specific.

The one gap I'd flag: Workshop 3 covers connections (what an agent can reach) but there's no slide directly borrowing from main deck slide 46 (the 6-icon connections grid). It's handled narratively in the exercises instead.

## Assessment of adapted slides

### AI Academy Pitch

**Slide 4 — Combined "1 billion+ / <2%" stat** (main: two separate bigNum slides 6–7)
> **Beneficial.** The two stats are stronger as a single contrast than two separate slides. A pitch deck doesn't have room for a slow reveal — compressing them sharpens the point.

**Slide 7 — Spectrum with "Most of us are here / The Academy takes you here"** (main: two slides, 4–5)
> **Beneficial.** The pitch needs to show the from/to in one visual. Combining the spectrum with both labels on the same slide communicates the product proposition cleanly. The main deck separates them for pacing; the pitch deck doesn't have that luxury.

---

### Workshop 1: Whisperer

**Slide 3 — "You've tried AI. Today you learn to talk to it properly."** (main: passive breather slide 11)
> **Beneficial.** The main deck's breather is subtle and positional. For a live workshop you need an explicit orientation beat — this does it cleanly.

**Slide 6 — "Most people type something vague and hope for the best."** (main: "The Task" slide 23)
> **Beneficial.** The main deck shows a chat bubble example; the workshop names the habit explicitly before the exercise. Good setup — participants know what they're about to recognise in themselves.

---

### Workshop 2: Strategist

**Slide 7 — "Same prompt. Now it sounds like someone who works here."** (main: before/after split slide 35)
> **Beneficial in context, but loses something.** The main deck shows actual output — you can read the difference. The workshop just states it. However, at this point participants have just done the exercise and experienced it themselves, so the live proof substitutes for the visual. Acceptable tradeoff.

**Slide 12 — "What if the AI already knew?"** (main: slide 41, follows "from scratch every time")
> **Beneficial.** In the workshop, participants have literally just pasted context manually, so the pain is fresh. The main deck has to manufacture that feeling; the workshop inherits it from the exercise. Better sequencing.

**Slide 21 — "But who's doing all the work? Still you."** (main: quiet breather slide 44)
> **Beneficial.** The main deck's breather is understated. As a live workshop closing cliffhanger, making it a punchy hero with the answer on screen ("Still you.") is more memorable. Sets up W3 better.

---

### Workshop 3: Operator

**Slide 4 — Copy-paste cycle as explicit 4-step list** (main: implied in slides 45–46)
> **Beneficial.** Externalising "1. You search, 2. You paste, 3. AI drafts, 4. You apply" makes the bottleneck pattern visible. Everyone recognises themselves before the Ferrari metaphor lands.

**Slide 6 — Generic agent-driven steps** (main: specific onboarding example in slide 47)
> **Beneficial.** The main deck ties the agent workflow to the onboarding scenario. The workshop needs to work for any task participants bring, so generalising is correct.

**Slide 10 — "An agent is only as useful as what it can reach."** (main: 6-icon connections grid, slide 46)
> **Loss worth addressing.** The main deck's connections grid gives people a mental inventory of what's possible (web, email, calendar, databases, files, MCP/custom). The workshop replaces it with a single hero statement and goes straight into a web research exercise. Participants learn that agents can search the web — but they don't learn the full range of connections. They leave without knowing about email, calendar, or database connections. Worth adding at minimum a brief list or the grid before the exercise.

**Slide 12 — "It found something you wouldn't have thought to provide."** (main: specific "forgotten budget memo" story)
> **Beneficial.** The main deck's story is vivid but specific to the onboarding scenario. In the workshop, participants have just done their own task and found their own version of this — the generic statement invites them to connect it to their own experience, which is more powerful.

**Slide 17 — "One agent... What if you had a whole team?"** (main: full kitchen brigade explanation, slide 52)
> **Beneficial.** Good restraint — plants the seed for W4 without giving away the metaphor. The full brigade concept is saved for where it belongs.

---

### Workshop 4: Orchestrator

**Slide 4 — "One chef doing a 10-course dinner alone?"** isolated as its own hero (main: part of longer brigade explanation, slide 52)
> **Beneficial.** Breaking the question out gives it room to land before the principle is stated. Better pacing than the main deck.

**Slide 12 — "Skills — You already know these. Now each agent gets its own."** (main: slide 56 explains skills from scratch)
> **Beneficial.** The most elegant adaptation in the suite. The main deck has to introduce skills as a concept; the workshop can just do a callback to W2. Respects the audience's learning and creates a satisfying through-line.

**Slide 19 — Full spectrum graduation with Explorer→Orchestrator highlighted** (main: callback narrative, slide 70)
> **Beneficial.** For a live 4-session programme, showing the full journey visually is far more impactful than a text callback. People who've been in the room for all four sessions can see exactly what they've covered. No equivalent in the main deck.

## Missed/miscategorised slides — corrections

### Whisperer Slide 4 — incorrectly called "directly borrowed"

**Main deck slide 15:**
```
"You use apps.
Apps use agents.
Agents call APIs.
APIs talk to LLMs."
```
**Workshop slide 4:**
```
"You use apps.
Apps use agents.
Agents talk to LLMs."
```
Two changes: (1) the API layer is dropped entirely — 4 lines become 3, (2) the sub "You only need to care about the first two" is moved from notes onto the slide itself.

**Assessment:** Both beneficial. The API layer is irrelevant to non-developers and adding it only creates a question to answer. Moving the "only the first two" guidance onto the slide makes the key takeaway explicit rather than a facilitator note. The slight inaccuracy (agents don't talk directly to LLMs, they go through APIs) is an acceptable simplification for the audience.

---

### Pitch Slide 4 — the `<2%` stat is reframed

**Main deck slide 7:** `"< 2% pay for premium"` (sourced to Reuters — the stat is about premium payment as a proxy for serious usage)

**Pitch deck:** `"Less than 2% use them well"` (same source, different claim)

**Assessment:** Problematic. "Pay for premium" and "use AI well" are related but not the same thing. The pitch version makes a stronger claim than the source actually supports — there are people who pay but use it poorly, and there are free-tier users who use it well. If anyone fact-checks the source, the wording won't match. Worth reverting to the original stat phrasing.

---

### Pitch Slide 5 — "19% longer" is degeneralised

**Main deck slide 19:** `"when developers rely on AI without understanding it"` — the study is specifically about software developers

**Pitch:** `"when you rely on AI without understanding it"` — generalised to any audience

**Assessment:** Reasonable intent, but slightly dishonest to the source. The Addy Osmani stat is specifically a coding productivity study. Applying it to general business workers overstates its scope. Acceptable for a pitch if caveatted, but the speaker should know this when presenting it.

---

### Strategist Slide 7 — before/after becomes a hero statement

**Main deck slide 35:** A split-column light-background slide showing "Plausible but generic / Could be for any company." vs "Sounds like someone who works here / Same prompt + your context." — same visual language as the wrong/right teaching slides.

**Workshop slide 7:** `hero("Same prompt. Now it sounds like someone who works here.")`

**Assessment:** Covered above — acceptable because participants have just done the exercise and seen the comparison live. But it's worth noting that the main deck's visual comparison makes the gap viscerally clear at a glance, which the hero statement doesn't.

---

### "Meet your AI colleague" slide — absent from all workshops

**Main deck slide 36:** A full slide with the Dory image + "Meet your AI colleague." — a visual moment before the "Brilliant. Helpful. Remembers nothing." punchline. It's the image that does the emotional work; the next slide lands the concept.

**All workshops:** Skip straight to "Brilliant. Helpful. Remembers nothing." with no image preamble.

**Assessment:** Minor loss. The image slide is a beat that sets the tone — humour signals this is approachable, not technical. Without it, the punchline arrives without setup. Low priority to fix since the image asset would need to travel with the workshop deck, but worth noting.
